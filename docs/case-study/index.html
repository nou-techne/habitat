Case Study: Pine Ridge Studio

A software cooperative's first quarter using Habitat's patronage accounting system. Real numbers, real decisions, real allocations.

Context: Pine Ridge is a 5-member software cooperative building open-source design tools. They formed as an LCA in January 2026 and adopted Habitat to track contributions and allocate patronage quarterly.

The Members

  • Alice — Lead developer, 30 hrs/week @ $60/hr
  • Bob — Designer & UX, 25 hrs/week @ $55/hr
  • Chen — DevOps & infrastructure, 20 hrs/week @ $65/hr
  • Dana — Community & documentation, 15 hrs/week @ $50/hr
  • Eli — Sales & partnerships, 10 hrs/week @ $70/hr

Community contributions: Each member participated in weekly planning meetings, code reviews, and onboarding a new member. Habitat tracked attendance and assigned equal community credit.

The Patronage Formula

Pine Ridge configured their patronage weights as:

  • 40% Labor (hours × rate)
  • 30% Revenue (client contracts secured)
  • 20% Cash (capital contributions)
  • 10% Community (meeting participation, mutual aid)

Total distributable surplus for Q1: $18,000 (after expenses, reserves, and community grant allocation).

Allocation Calculation

Alice's Patronage Share

  • Labor: $21,600 / $71,100 × 40% = 12.15%
  • Revenue: $0 / $45,000 × 30% = 0%
  • Cash: $5,000 / $15,000 × 20% = 6.67%
  • Community: 20% (equal share) × 10% = 2%

Total Patronage: 20.82%

Allocated Surplus: 20.82% × $18,000 = $3,747

What the System Tracked

Behind these numbers, Habitat recorded:

  • 187 contribution events (time entries, cash transfers, revenue bookings, meeting attendance)
  • Capital account movements for each member (contributions in, allocations out)
  • Book and tax ledgers synchronized (704(b) compliance maintained)
  • Audit trail — every event timestamped and immutable

The Decision That Mattered

In March, the cooperative discussed whether to weight revenue contributions at 30% or 40%. Eli argued for 40% (favoring his sales role). Alice and Bob preferred 30% (more weight on labor). Dana abstained.

The vote: 3 for 30%, 1 for 40%, 1 abstention. The cooperative kept the 30% weight.

Key insight: Habitat didn't make this decision — the members did. The system just made the consequences transparent. With 40%, Eli would have received $7,811 instead of $6,611. With 30%, Alice and Bob's shares increased.

Governance shapes allocation. The tools make the trade-offs visible.

Next Quarter

Pine Ridge is considering:

  • Adding a "code quality" factor (weighting code reviews and test coverage)
  • Splitting labor into "billable" and "internal" categories with different weights
  • Increasing the community factor to 15% to recognize mutual aid more heavily

The cooperative can reconfigure patronage weights each quarter through a governance vote. Habitat adapts — the formula is policy, not hardcode.

Lessons

  1. Transparency enables debate. Members argued about weights because they could see the impact. Opacity prevents negotiation.
  2. Multi-capital accounting works. Labor, revenue, cash, and community contributions all mattered. Single-metric systems (e.g., "equity split by hours") would have missed the full picture.
  3. Patronage ≠ payroll. Allocations are distributions of surplus after expenses, not wages. Pine Ridge paid members monthly stipends (recorded as draws against future allocations) and reconciled quarterly.
  4. The system is as fair as the governance. Habitat calculates. The cooperative decides what to calculate.

This case study is illustrative. Names, numbers, and decisions are hypothetical but grounded in real cooperative accounting principles and Habitat's design.
